NATE 2024

Ultibot
Frisbee Launcher

Have you ever wanted to toss a frisbee around but didn't have a tossing partner? Then this project is for you!

This project was essentially a 2 week sprint on designing and building a mechatronics device. In the two weeks, I used 6 different manufacturing techniques, some include: CNC routing, waterjet, sheet metal bending, welding, FDM 3D printing, etc.

The frisbee launcher features 2 rotational degrees of freedom controlled by a linear actuator and a stepper motor. It has a brushed DC motor running at 5000 RPM. This launcher has the capabilities of launching a frisbee at 140 km/hr!

The overall objective of this project is to create a frisbee launcher that is capable of detecting a person’s position using computer vision, adjusting to the person’s position using two axis motion, and launching to the person’s right hand. The primary mechanical objectives of this project is the ability to have a structure capable of launching a frisbee at high speeds and housing the electrical components. The primary software objectives of this project is the ability to have autonomous closed looped computer vision control of the device. The primary electrical objectives of this project is the ability to power the movement and actuation of the device. The primary firmware objectives of this project is the ability to control the movement and actuation of the device. Through these four subsystem objectives, the Ultibot achieves its primary objective of launching a frisbee.

TESLA
POWERWALL 3

During my 9 month internship with Tesla, I worked on creating hardware for Powerwall 3. As an Energy Hardware Mechanical Design Intern at Tesla, I worked on designing the rapid shutdown switch for the Powerwall, which is intended to be produced in quantities of 1 million per year. My innovative approach solved supply chain, ergonomic, and waterproofing challenges. By optimizing the injection-molded design, I achieved an 800% reduction in assembly force, an 80% size reduction, and reduced the part count from 15 to just 4 components. All critical features underwent tolerance stack analysis, achieving a 5 sigma confidence in assembly, and I conducted an FMEA analysis for validation testing requirements.

Project Name

As a Soft Robotics Researcher at UBC Micro-electrical Mechanical Systems (MEMS) lab, I was tasked with designing a novel pneumatic soft actuator using thermoelastic shape-memory-polymer (SMP), resulting in a proof-of-concept design from start to finish. The most challenging aspect was the selection of the SMP material due to the lack of available data. By formulating 10 different compositions of SMPs, I characterized their material properties at varying temperatures using a dynamic mechanical analyzer. I opted to conduct 8 different FEA simulations to identify the material and geometry that best showcased the actuator's capability of multiple trajectories without failure. This decision eliminated the trial and error of prototyping, accelerating this project phase by 2 weeks. The additional time allowed us to prepare and publish a paper at a soft robotics conference, which received positive feedback, as the actuator demonstrated twice the number of trajectories compared to a traditional pneumatic soft actuator.
